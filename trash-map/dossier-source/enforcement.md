# Enforcement & Data RACI — Inspection, Sanctions, Tracking, Audit

> **Two time horizons + a speculative tier.** This matrix now spans **pre-new-law (current state, 2024-2026)** and **post-new-law (2027+)** activities, plus a **speculative tier** for citizen-proposed institutions that surface in survey evidence but are not in current or new-law architecture. The new Law on Integrated Waste Management adds a National Waste Information System (NWIS), mandatory reporting for municipalities/operators/PROs, strengthened inspection powers, and a Hybrid Regulator (HReg) for tariff and performance audit (per Mazreku/MMPHI deck, PR #13). Rows that describe post-new-law activities are tagged **(new-law)** inline. Rows marked ⚠ flag citizen-driven gaps where there is no clean A in current formal channels.

> Layer 3 of 3. The "phantom A" layer — accountability assigned but rarely activated. ~80% confident on who has the authority; less confident on who actually exercises it. Most pre-new-law rows have a documented A and an absent R. New-law rows are ~60% confident — sourced from a single deck (Mazreku/MMPHI) and describe institutions that don't yet exist.

See [`README.md`](README.md) for marker conventions and full actor codes.

## Actors in this matrix

MMPHI · **HReg** · AMMK · KOM · INSP · POL · COURT · OMB · PRO · NGO · PRESS · CIT

**New actor codes (introduced by new Law on Integrated Waste Management):**

- **HReg — Hybrid Regulator.** New independent regulator created by the new law. In the enforcement context: tariff dispute resolution and performance non-compliance audit across the waste sector. Does not yet exist; expected operational from 2027 once secondary legislation passes. Until then, cells in the HReg column read "—" for current-state rows.
- **PRO — Producer Responsibility Organizations.** Written into law (EPR/DRS); not yet operationally instantiated. Listed here because the new law makes PROs mandatory reporters into NWIS and audited parties under EPR/DRS compliance.

## Row notes

**Row 1 — Issue licenses.** MMPHI has clean A,R. Notably, **MMPHI has not used licensing as a sanction mechanism** even when Pastrimi service failures were severe. The authority exists; the muscle doesn't.

**Row 2 — Inspect operators.** MMPHI is A; municipal inspectors are R. But: municipal inspectors are politically constrained from fining the public enterprise their Municipality owns. The A and R are misaligned in incentive — inspectors work for KOM, KOM is Pastrimi's shareholder.

**Row 3 — Inspect landfills.** AMMK is the operational R (environmental monitoring). They've publicly stated Kosovo landfills lack leachate treatment, integrated environmental permits, daily cover compliance. **They report; they don't enforce.** No A is exercised.

**Row 4 — Fine households.** Komuna A, municipal inspectors R. Police can also issue fines (C). Per Atlas Institute: enforcement is "sporadic," targets visible offenders, rarely households.

**Row 5 — Fine construction sites.** Same authority structure as row 4. **In practice, near-zero enforcement** even though construction is the documented largest source of illegal dumping. Pastrimi has reported names + addresses to authorities; no further action taken (per Springer 2023 study).

**Row 6 — Sanction operators.** MMPHI A. Tools available include license revocation, regulatory sanctions. **Not used against Pastrimi during the 2024-25 crisis** despite documented service failures. Phantom A.

**Row 7 — Illegal-dumpsite register.** AMMK official count: 458 (2024), 403 (2023). Civil society + joint AMMK/GIZ studies: 1,572 (2021), 500+ (Let's Do It Kosovo current). The methodological gap is informative — different counts serve different purposes.

**Row 8 — National waste-flow data.** AMMK runs the system. INDEP/KAS Recommendation 1 calls for a new national material-flow database — i.e., the current system is inadequate. Row 15 (NWIS) is the post-2027 successor / formalization with mandatory reporting.

**Row 9 — Audit EPR/DRS.** Conditional on those systems operating. Currently ? in practice (issues #4, #5). This is the *current-state* row; row 17 is the post-new-law version where HReg becomes A,R.

**Row 10 — Adjudicate disputes.** Courts are clean A,R. Supreme Court ruled June 2025 in Pastrimi's favor on the regulation dispute. Constitutional Court referral status unclear. Note: post-2027, in-sector tariff and performance disputes route to HReg (see policy.md row 21); courts remain backup for criminal and constitutional matters.

**Row 11 — Environmental health risks.** NIPHK/IKSHPK has authority but rarely activates. The Feb 2025 emergency was politically declared, not NIPHK-driven.

**Row 12 — Receive citizen reports (current channels).** Pre-existing row, retained. Captures the *current* analog/phone-based reporting paths through KOM and INSP. The DYVÓ citizen survey (n=150) found citizens **did not know where to report illegal waste** — some guessed police, some guessed inspection services, **none had ever reported illegal dumping**. The gap that the Trash Reporter app (HackTheTrash) is filling. **See row 16** for the structured-digital-reports extension and **row 15** for how NWIS could give those reports a formal data home.

**Row 13 — Public watchdog.** Civil society is doing the work that AMMK should be doing. Let's Do It Kosovo, INDEP, Atlas Institute, BIRN-affiliated outlets all publish independent counts and critiques.

**Row 14 — Press accountability.** KOHA, Telegrafi, Prishtina Insight, Balkan Insight, RFE/RL all cover the waste beat. They're the de facto enforcement of accountability when formal channels don't act.

**Row 15 — (new-law) Operate the NWIS.** Per Mazreku/MMPHI deck §7 (slide 11), the new law establishes a National Waste Information System with **mandatory reporting** by municipalities, operators, and PROs, plus strengthened inspection powers and regular performance/compliance audits. MMPHI is A (policy ownership); AMMK is operational R (continues data role from row 8); KOM, operators, and PROs are all R as mandatory reporters — the regime depends on each feeding clean data in. HReg is C (uses NWIS data for tariff and performance functions). Citizens are I in this row; row 16 is where citizen input flows into the architecture if a structured channel is built.

**Row 16 — Receive structured citizen reports (digital) ⚠.** New row. The DYVÓ citizen research surfaced an explicit gap: citizens have no formal channel for digital, structured, evidence-bearing reports. Currently NO clean A in formal channels — KOM and INSP are marked ⚠ to flag that authority *should* live there but does not in practice. The Trash Reporter app (HackTheTrash) is positioned as the proposed R for intake. Per the Mazreku/MMPHI deck §11, the Reporter app could be designed as **a citizen-facing front-end to NWIS** (row 15), turning citizen reports into a structured input to state data infrastructure rather than a parallel system. *Cross-reference any open Reporter-app verification issue when filed.*

**Row 17 — (new-law) Audit EPR/DRS compliance.** Post-new-law operational regime. Conditional on EPR and DRS systems operating (verification issues #4, #5). HReg is A,R for performance non-compliance audit; MMPHI is C on policy framework; PROs and operators are R as mandatory reporters subject to audit. This is the post-2027 successor to row 9 and shifts audit accountability from MMPHI's "phantom A" to a dedicated regulator — *if* HReg stands up with capacity.

**Row 18 — Investigate cross-municipal waste flows.** Emerges from the dossier's system-map data plus DYVÓ's "bin tourism" finding (cross-neighborhood + cross-municipal waste movement that GPS evidence could expose). MMPHI is A as policy owner; AMMK is R for the data side; KOM is C (each municipality owns part of the picture). WMZ-level cooperation is needed but not yet operational — the new law's Five Waste Management Zones will eventually be the structural answer here, but until 2027 there is no operational R for cross-jurisdiction flow investigation.

**Row 19 — "Green Police" unit (speculative).** Citizen-proposed in the DYVÓ survey: when respondents were asked about enforcement, some suggested a dedicated "police" function for environmental violations, distinct from regular police. The proposal is *real evidence of citizen demand* for an enforcement gap-filler, even though no such unit is in current or new-law architecture. **Why citizens propose it:** rows 4, 5, and 12 are the phantom-A vacuum citizens experience day-to-day — illegal dumping is unfined, reports go nowhere, and existing inspectors are politically constrained (row 2 note). **What gap it would fill:** a unit with both visibility (uniformed presence) and authority (issue fines, not just refer them) for environmental violations. **Why current institutions could fill it but don't:** municipal inspectors (INSP) already have the legal authority for rows 4-5; police (POL) already have the visibility. The combined function is a coordination problem, not a legal-authority gap. Marked **?A** because no actor is currently assigned and the proposal is not legislated. Included in the matrix because shouting "build a new institution" is itself a finding about the perceived legitimacy of existing ones.

## What this matrix shows

- **Almost every pre-new-law row has a clean A on paper** — somebody is theoretically accountable.
- **Most R cells are weakly exercised.** "Has authority" ≠ "does the work."
- **Courts and press are the only actors with both A and active R.** Both are reactive (case-by-case, story-by-story), not systematic.
- **Civil society fills the watchdog vacuum** that AMMK + Ombudsperson should fill.
- **Citizens are the unrecognized R on reporting illegal dumping.** No formal channel respects them as a node — and the new structured-reporting row (16) makes that absence explicit.
- **The new law addresses the phantom-A architecturally.** NWIS (row 15), HReg as audit A,R (row 17), and mandatory reporting upgrade the formal scaffolding. Whether secondary legislation in 2027 actually gives it teeth is the open empirical question.
- **Citizen-proposed "Green Police" is a tell.** Citizens reach for new institutions because the existing ones feel inert. Diagnostic value > policy proposal.

## Where the Reporter app fits (HackTheTrash)

The app slots into rows 12 and 16 specifically. Row 12 captures the analog-channel status quo; row 16 is the structured-digital extension where the app provides the intake R that no formal actor currently owns. The authority-routing layer (the "auto-route to the right authority" feature) connects citizen R into the inspector R chain (row 2). Building this also pressures rows 4 + 5 (fining illegal dumpers) since the app produces evidence — photos, GPS, timestamps — that current enforcement lacks. Post-2027, the app could become a citizen-facing front-end to NWIS (row 15), and row-18 cross-municipal-flow analytics become a derivable feature from aggregated GPS reports.

## What's missing or unclear

- **No row for prosecutorial authority.** The Public Prosecutor's office has authority for criminal environmental cases but is rarely activated. Ombudsperson Jashari has noted "the prosecution and the courts are not giving priority to any cases related to the environment." Worth adding once verified.
- **Worker safety + occupational health** for waste operators is not modeled here.
- **Cross-jurisdiction inspection** between KOM and surrounding municipalities is partially captured by row 18 but operational R remains absent until WMZ stands up.
- **Strengthened inspection powers** in the new law (Mazreku/MMPHI §7) are not yet decomposed into rows — depends on secondary legislation 2027 to know what new powers attach to which actor.
- **HReg standing-up risk.** Three new-law accountabilities cluster in HReg (tariff disputes per policy row 21, EPR/DRS audit per row 17 here, NWIS performance use). Single-point-of-failure for post-2027 enforcement architecture.
