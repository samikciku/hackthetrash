## Part 1: The physical system

### What waste exists and where it goes

Kosovo generates approximately 490,000 tons of municipal solid waste per year (2023 figure). Per capita waste generation is approximately 0.73 kg per inhabitant per day. Waste collection coverage nationally is approximately 50-85% depending on which methodology and area used: roughly 85% for households, 54% for businesses, 85% for public institutions ([Heinrich Böll Stiftung 2022](https://rs.boell.org/en/2022/08/03/kosovos-waste-mismanagement-failures-alternatives); [Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)).

Of waste that is collected and reaches managed disposal, approximately 92.8% goes to one of seven public landfills. Approximately 3% is recycled, mostly by private businesses operating in the informal or semi-formal sector ([Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)). The remainder ends up in illegal dumpsites, of which Kosovo has somewhere between 458 (Kosovo Environmental Protection Agency count, [Balkan Insight 2025](https://balkaninsight.com/2025/11/28/unbearable-the-legal-and-illegal-landfills-harming-lives-and-land-in-kosovo/)) and over 1,500 (other estimates including [BIRN's regional mapping project, 2024](https://balkaninsight.com/2024/03/29/its-devastating-birn-maps-illegal-landfills-blighting-the-balkans/)). The discrepancy itself is informative: nobody actually has authoritative data on illegal dumping, and the count varies by methodology and political incentive.

For 2023, Kosovo ranked as the third worst country globally for unsorted household waste according to the Lloyd's Register Foundation World Risk Poll ([Balkan Insight / Prishtina Insight 2024](https://balkaninsight.com/2024/09/17/kosovo-montenegro-albania-among-worlds-worst-in-household-waste-disposal-report/)).

### The seven public landfills

Five regional landfills serve the major population regions ([Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/); [WasteCulture 2021](https://wasteculture.com/paper/4412/Current-state-of-Waste-Management-in-Kosovo-2021.html)):
- **Mirash** (Pristina region)
- **Gërmova** (Mitrovica region)
- **Sferkë** (Pejë/Pec region)
- **Landovicë** (Prizren region)
- **Velekincë** (Gjilan region)

Two additional landfills serve specific municipalities: Podujevë and Dragash.

Four additional sites are categorized as non-sanitary: Istog, Zubin Potok, Leposaviq, and Zveqan.

All seven public landfills were built between 2001 and 2008 with EU support. All have exceeded their designed capacity. None possess wastewater (leachate) treatment infrastructure. None have integrated environmental permits, despite KLMC applying for them ([Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)). Most operate beyond their planned lifespan ([RFE/RL 2024](https://www.rferl.org/a/pollution-balkans-kosovo/33210735.html)).

### Mirash specifically

The Mirash landfill is the disposal site for waste from the Pristina region. Built 2004-2006 at a cost of approximately 3.5 million euros ([EU enlargement action summary](https://enlargement.ec.europa.eu/document/download/8deaf178-57e2-41aa-94bc-b5b710853e1e_en)). Located approximately 10 km west of Pristina in the village of Mirash, in Obiliq/Obilic municipality. The site sits on land owned by Kosovo Energy Corporation (KEK), in a former coal pit. The landfill began operating in December 2006.

Mirash receives waste from at least six Kosovar cities, totaling approximately 160,000 metric tons per year ([RFE/RL 2024](https://www.rferl.org/a/pollution-balkans-kosovo/33210735.html)). An older Prishtina Insight report from 2017 noted that approximately one third of all waste produced in Kosovo (around 100,000 tons in 2015) ended up at Mirash, with the landfill described as servicing seven municipalities: Prishtina, Drenas, Lipjan, Podujeva, Gracanica, Obilic and Fushe Kosove ([Prishtina Insight 2017](https://prishtinainsight.com/mirash-landfill-looming-environmental-disaster-mag/)).

The site sits lower than a nearby lake, leaving it susceptible to flooding. Sewage flooding has been an ongoing problem for years. KLMC director Dardan Velija (then KMDK director) confirmed: "The problem with sewage at this landfill has been out of control for many years because the collection point has flooded" ([RFE/RL 2024](https://www.rferl.org/a/pollution-balkans-kosovo/33210735.html)).

The 2017 Prishtina Insight investigation provides the most detailed environmental account: groundwater analyses showed pollution spreading to underground premises of the landfill; the original leachate management plan recommended a recirculation system relying on evaporation, which failed within one year of opening; the "landfill lake" rises by over one meter each year; spontaneous methane fires occur from inappropriate coal deposit; no degassing system has been installed. Ombudsperson Hilmi Jashari warned the situation could lead to a methane explosion accident similar to the 1993 Turkey landfill disaster ([Prishtina Insight 2017](https://prishtinainsight.com/mirash-landfill-looming-environmental-disaster-mag/)).

The surrounding area also hosts Kosovo's coal-fired power plants (Kosovo A and Kosovo B), making it one of the most polluted areas in the country. Residents report unbearable summer odors, plastic-burning smell, and clusters of cancers. Activists assert connections between Mirash, the KEK power plants, and elevated cancer rates among nearby residents ([RFE/RL 2024](https://www.rferl.org/a/pollution-balkans-kosovo/33210735.html); [Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)).

Closure of Mirash and construction of a new Pristina-region landfill is an EU-flagged immediate priority ([EU enlargement action summary](https://enlargement.ec.europa.eu/document/download/8deaf178-57e2-41aa-94bc-b5b710853e1e_en)). Funding studies exist. Closure has not happened.

### Composition and behavior of the waste stream

Waste in Kosovo is largely unsorted. There is no national waste sorting or treatment plant ([WasteCulture 2021](https://wasteculture.com/paper/4412/Current-state-of-Waste-Management-in-Kosovo-2021.html)). Some municipalities have piloted source separation. Recycling at scale is conducted by private operators (Izolimi Plast Recycling, Kosovo Recycling Glass, Rec-Kos, others) and an informal sector of scrap collectors who pull recyclables out of bins before pickup ([Prishtina Insight 2023 / Waste Warriors of Necessity](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-warriors-of-necessity-informal-waste-pickers-carving-a-livelihood-from-scraps-mag/)).

Construction and demolition waste (CDW) is a major problem. Kosovo generates approximately 167,900 tons of CDW annually according to MMPHI ([academic study, Pristina/Fushe Kosova case](https://www.academia.edu/145160740/Construction_and_Demolition_Waste_in_Kosovo_A_case_studyin_the_municipalities_of_Pristina_and_Fushe_Kosova); [Springer/Journal of Material Cycles and Waste Management 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10163-022-01577-5)). The vibrant Kosovo construction sector, particularly in urban areas, is poorly controlled, and CDW is the primary driver of illegal dumping along roads and river edges ([EU enlargement action summary](https://enlargement.ec.europa.eu/document/download/8deaf178-57e2-41aa-94bc-b5b710853e1e_en)). CDW often gets mixed with municipal waste at landfills because no dedicated CDW facilities exist. The Mirash landfill does not officially accept CDW, though small quantities are sometimes accepted to build retaining walls and secure foundations. Construction sites pay approximately 80 euros per 8-10 cubic meter container for CDW removal, often handled by unlicensed private operators ([Springer 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10163-022-01577-5)).

Hazardous and medical waste is essentially unmanaged at the national level. No dedicated facilities for hazardous waste exist. Medical waste theoretically goes through separate streams but in practice often mixes with municipal waste ([US Trade.gov / Kosovo Waste Management and Recycling](https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/kosovo-waste-management-and-recycling); [Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)).

### The informal recovery economy

The "scrap collectors" or informal waste pickers are a significant informal labor force, predominantly Roma, Ashkali, and Egyptian (RAE) community members. They work the bins in the early morning, pulling metal, plastic, paper, and glass before Pastrimi trucks arrive. They sell to recyclers and aggregators who feed the small formal recycling sector ([Prishtina Insight 2023](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-warriors-of-necessity-informal-waste-pickers-carving-a-livelihood-from-scraps-mag/)).

The Prishtina Insight in-depth feature on this community is the most detailed source available. Workers like Turkian Iseni (24, Roma, Fushë Kosova) have been collecting since age 7. A typical day yields approximately 100 kilograms of recyclables for approximately 15 euros. Buyers like Rec-Kos (the largest recycling firm in Fushë Kosova) export most processed materials to steel factories in Turkey and Greece. Attempts to formalize the work have repeatedly failed because formalization would mean losing unemployment benefits and exposing collectors to taxes and inspections ([Prishtina Insight 2023](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-warriors-of-necessity-informal-waste-pickers-carving-a-livelihood-from-scraps-mag/)).

Their work is technically unlicensed and falls in a legal grey area, but enforcement is zero because they are doing work the state is not doing. They are also often blamed for tearing open bags and spreading waste around containers, which is true but downstream of the structural problem (containers are too small and too few, so bags spill out regardless).

The Ombudsperson, Hilmi Jashari, has previously commented on the rights and conditions of scrap collectors. The community is economically vulnerable and politically invisible.

## Part 2: The actors

### Operational core

#### Pastrimi (KRM Pastrimi JSC)

The Regional Waste Collection Company "Pastrimi" Joint Stock Company. A public enterprise with approximately 800-900 employees that provides waste collection services for several cities in Kosovo, with Pristina as its primary territory ([Cna.al/Kosova-Bota 2025](https://www.cna.al/english/kosova-bota/komuna-dhe-pastrimi-perplasen-per-faturat-e-grumbullimit-te-mbeturin-i422082)). The Municipality of Pristina is the main shareholder.

Pastrimi operates 14 trucks for household waste collection and 3 trucks for businesses in Pristina, with 10 more being purchased ([Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)). Japan's JICA grant aid program has provided 10 new compactor trucks to Pastrimi ([JICA Kosovo](https://www.jica.go.jp/kosovo/english/activities/activity05.html)).

Pastrimi is a self-financing enterprise, meaning it is not part of the municipal budget and covers all its expenses (salaries, fuel, vehicle maintenance, landfill fees) from collected revenue ([Telegrafi April 2025 interview with Reçica](https://telegrafi.com/en/mospajtimet-se-ku-duhet-te-paguhen-faturat-e-mbeturinave-ne-prishtine-flet-kryeshefi-ndermarrjes-pastrimi/)).

CEO: **Petrit Reçica**. Has been the public face of Pastrimi throughout the 2024-2025 crisis. Defends the December 2024 regulation aggressively, frames the dispute as the Municipality attempting to bankrupt Pastrimi to justify private operator contracts. Frequently quoted in [Telegrafi](https://telegrafi.com/en/mospajtimet-se-ku-duhet-te-paguhen-faturat-e-mbeturinave-ne-prishtine-flet-kryeshefi-ndermarrjes-pastrimi/), [KOHA](https://www.koha.net/en/lajmet-e-mbremjes-ktv/pastrimi-kerkon-nga-komuna-tia-ktheje-parate-qe-i-mori-nga-qytetaret), and other outlets. Voice tends to be formal, defensive of legal rights, mixing institutional language with direct accusations.

Workers' Union president: **Ardian Krasniqi**. Public face of the union during the May-June 2025 strikes. Voice is more direct than Reçica's, often combative. Explicitly opposes private operators and frames the conflict as protecting public sector employment ([KOHA March 2026](https://www.koha.net/en/lajmet-e-mbremjes-ktv/pastrimi-kerkon-nga-komuna-tia-ktheje-parate-qe-i-mori-nga-qytetaret)).

Pastrimi's client register reportedly contains approximately 95,000 clients (Pastrimi's count) versus approximately 72,000 (Municipality's count) ([KOHA April 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/qytetaret-ne-prishtine-vazhdojne-te-jene-ne-dileme-se-ku-ti-paguajne-tarifat-e-mbeturinave)). The 23,000-client gap is a flashpoint: Pastrimi argues the Municipality has refused to register approximately 25,000 new clients since 2023, costing Pastrimi an estimated 3.5 million euros in 2024 alone ([KOHA January 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/prishtinasit-do-te-faturohen-dyfish-per-mbeturinat)).

Pastrimi charges approximately 6.20 euros/month per household with VAT. This is a flat fee, not metered. There is no pay-as-you-throw mechanism ([Balkan Insight 2025](https://balkaninsight.com/2025/11/28/unbearable-the-legal-and-illegal-landfills-harming-lives-and-land-in-kosovo/)).

#### KLMC (Kosovo Landfill Management Company)

Also written as KMDK (Kompania për Menaxhimin e Deponive të Kosovës) in Albanian-language sources. The publicly owned company responsible for supervising and managing waste disposal sites, including Mirash. Operates under the Ministry of Economy ([US Trade.gov](https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/kosovo-waste-management-and-recycling)).

Director: **Dardan Velija** (current). Previous director: Abdullah Haxhiu (as cited in [Prishtina Insight 2017 Mirash article](https://prishtinainsight.com/mirash-landfill-looming-environmental-disaster-mag/)).

KLMC operates Mirash and most other public landfills. Charges entrance fees that, per EU assessment, only cover basic landfill management costs and do not provide for subsequent care or remediation ([Balkan Insight 2025](https://balkaninsight.com/2025/11/28/unbearable-the-legal-and-illegal-landfills-harming-lives-and-land-in-kosovo/)).

KLMC says Pastrimi owes it approximately 1.9 million euros in unpaid landfill fees ([Balkan Insight 2025](https://balkaninsight.com/2025/11/28/unbearable-the-legal-and-illegal-landfills-harming-lives-and-land-in-kosovo/)). This debt is one of the structural pressure points: if KLMC refuses to accept Pastrimi trucks, collection in Pristina effectively stops, but KLMC also depends on Pastrimi's payments for its own operating revenue.

#### Pastrimi's workers

Approximately 800-900 employees ([Cna.al 2025](https://www.cna.al/english/kosova-bota/komuna-dhe-pastrimi-perplasen-per-faturat-e-grumbullimit-te-mbeturin-i422082); [Ombudsperson statement May 2025](https://oik-rks.org/en/2025/05/19/the-ombudsperson-met-with-the-ceo-of-the-company-pastrimi-the-mayor-of-pristina-was-absent/)). Primary collection workers, drivers, sorting and dispatch staff. Heavily unionized. Politically aligned with the ruling Assembly bloc that protects Pastrimi (PDK).

Workers struck in May-June 2025 protesting both the Municipality's withholding of payments and the contracting of private operators by the Municipality. Worker quoted in [KOHA June 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/lajmet-e-mbremjes-ktv/punetoret-e-krm-pastrimi-sdalin-ne-terren-sduan-ti-ndajne-punet-me-kompani-te-tjera): "We have not asked for anything, just to be allowed to do our job. We have been doing this job for many years. Don't let them stop us. We are tired of the tricks they play among themselves."

Strike posture: Workers refuse to share collection territory with private operators. They are willing to protest in front of the company building. They explicitly distrust both Municipality and management at points of high tension.

### Municipal level

#### Municipality of Pristina

The capital city's local government. Population of city proper approximately 200,000; municipality approximately 220,000. Main shareholder of Pastrimi.

The Municipality is currently led by **Mayor Përparim Rama** (LDK). Rama won the runoff in late 2025 and the Municipal Assembly was constituted in early 2026, but as of January 2026 he had not yet presented his governing cabinet ([KOHA January 2026](https://www.koha.net/en/038-ktv/pa-buxhet-e-me-protesta-viti-2025-per-prishtinen)). The deputy mayor position has rotated through several people including Alban Zogaj (left to become an MP), Florian Dushi (interim), and most recently Shkëmb Manaj.

The Municipality's position throughout the 2024-2025 crisis has been:
- The December 2024 regulation transferring billing authority to Pastrimi was passed by an opposition bloc (PDK and VV) and is illegitimate
- Citizens should pay waste fees to the Municipality, not Pastrimi
- The Municipality intends to bring in private operators (the cancelled but never-rescinded tender for five private operators)
- Pastrimi cannot be allowed exclusive operation because it cannot deliver service quality

Director of Public Services (during early 2025): **Bekim Brestovci**. Quoted in [KOHA January 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/prishtinasit-do-te-faturohen-dyfish-per-mbeturinat): "I apologize to the citizens who are facing confusion because it is true that 'Pastrimi', citing the changes to the regulation made by the municipal assembly, has started collecting invoices for 2025, while we as a municipality, due to legal uncertainty, have not stopped regenerating invoices for 2025." Subsequently replaced after national elections.

Municipality slogan for 2025: "Prishtina the cleanest city in the region" ([KOHA January 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/prishtinasit-do-te-faturohen-dyfish-per-mbeturinat)).

#### Pristina Municipal Assembly

The legislative body of the municipality. Composition during the December 2024 vote that triggered the regulation crisis: PDK and VV held a working majority and used it to amend the waste regulation, granting Pastrimi exclusive billing authority over Mayor Rama's (LDK) objections ([KOHA April 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/qytetaret-ne-prishtine-vazhdojne-te-jene-ne-dileme-se-ku-ti-paguajne-tarifat-e-mbeturinave)).

Current composition: shifted somewhat after the local elections, but the basic political dynamic remains: LDK holds the mayor's office, but PDK with VV support can outvote them on regulation-level issues.

The Assembly's December 2024 regulation amendment is the precipitating legal instrument. It modified the existing Waste Management Regulation No. 01-030/01-227581/23 (originally dated 01.12.2023), with the amendment recorded as no. 01-030/01-161704/24, dated 06.12.2024 ([KOHA April 2025 / KRM Pastrimi statement](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/krm-pastrimi-thote-se-shkaku-i-thirrjeve-te-komunes-se-prishtines-borxhi-ka-arritur-ne-22-milione-euro)). The amendment specifically transferred waste billing authority from the Municipality to Pastrimi.

#### Municipal inspectors and Kosovo Police

Municipal inspectors are responsible for enforcement of waste regulations at the municipal level: illegal dumping fines, commercial container misuse, construction site violations.

Kosovo Police can also issue fines for waste-related offenses, though enforcement is sporadic. Most fine enforcement targets visible offenders and rarely targets ordinary households. Almost no enforcement targets Pastrimi for service failures. Atlas Institute policy analyst Agron Demi has flagged that authorities rarely enforce penalties even when they have the authority ([Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)).

### National level

#### Ministry of Environment, Spatial Planning and Infrastructure (MMPHI)

The national ministry responsible for environmental policy, including waste. Drafts policies, strategies, and laws. Issues operating licenses to waste operators.

In May 2021, MMPHI adopted the new Kosovo Integrated Waste Management Strategy for 2021-2030, addressing current shortfalls and constraints in waste management with the aim to protect public health, reduce environmental impact, and transition toward a circular economy ([US Trade.gov](https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/kosovo-waste-management-and-recycling)).

In the Pastrimi dispute, MMPHI formally approved the Municipal Assembly's December 2024 regulation amendment as being "in line with the Law on Waste." This approval is one reason the regulation has held up against Municipal challenges ([KOHA April 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/qytetaret-ne-prishtine-vazhdojne-te-jene-ne-dileme-se-ku-ti-paguajne-tarifat-e-mbeturinave)).

The Ministry has not used its licensing authority to sanction Pastrimi for service failures, despite this being theoretically possible. Notably, MMPHI did not respond to BIRN's request for comment for the major 2024 illegal landfills mapping article ([Balkan Insight March 2024](https://balkaninsight.com/2024/03/29/its-devastating-birn-maps-illegal-landfills-blighting-the-balkans/)).

#### Kosovo Environmental Protection Agency (AMMK)

Also written as KEPA in some English sources. Environmental monitoring agency. Maintains the official register of illegal landfills (count: 458 nationally, [contested by other sources](https://balkaninsight.com/2025/11/28/unbearable-the-legal-and-illegal-landfills-harming-lives-and-land-in-kosovo/)). Per AMMK's own data, "in 2018, Kosovo had 2,246 illegal landfills. By 2023, this number had dropped to 403... Prizren has the highest number of illegal landfills, with 187 recorded sites, while Gjakovë/Djakovica has the lowest, with 37" ([Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)).

AMMK has stated that Kosovo landfills do not have wastewater treatment, have high harmful gas emissions, are accompanied by stray dogs, birds, and insects spreading infection, have insufficient fencing, and fail to cover waste daily despite legal obligation. AMMK has confirmed that no Kosovo sanitary landfill has an integrated environmental permit, despite KLMC applications ([Prishtina Insight 2025](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-management-a-systemic-crisis-in-kosovo-mag/)).

A separate joint AMMK/GIZ study identified approximately 1,572 illegal dumpsites across 38 municipalities as of 2021 ([WasteCulture 2021](https://wasteculture.com/paper/4412/Current-state-of-Waste-Management-in-Kosovo-2021.html); [Heinrich Böll 2022](https://rs.boell.org/en/2022/08/03/kosovos-waste-mismanagement-failures-alternatives)). The methodology gap with current AMMK figures explains some of the data dispute.

AMMK voice tends to be technical and bureaucratic. They produce reports. They do not enforce.

#### National Institute of Public Health of Kosovo (NIPHK / IKSHPK)

Investigates environmental health hazards, disease outbreaks, contamination risks. According to NIPHK, "improper waste management in Kosovo creates ideal conditions for the development of bacteria, parasites and insects, increasing the risk of infectious diseases such as dysentery, hepatitis A, cholera and respiratory tract infections" ([Balkan Insight 2025](https://balkaninsight.com/2025/11/28/unbearable-the-legal-and-illegal-landfills-harming-lives-and-land-in-kosovo/)).

Has authority to declare public health risks but rarely triggers enforcement. The Pristina Municipality cited "significant risk of a public health crisis" to justify the February 2025 emergency declaration; this was politically rather than NIPHK-driven.

#### Constitutional Court of Kosovo

The Municipality of Pristina sent the December 2024 regulation amendment to the Constitutional Court for review ([KOHA April 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/qytetaret-ne-prishtine-vazhdojne-te-jene-ne-dileme-se-ku-ti-paguajne-tarifat-e-mbeturinave)). As of mid-2025, the Court had not issued a decision. The case may have been resolved or remain pending; current status uncertain in available sources.

#### Supreme Court of Kosovo

Issued the consequential June 2025 ruling that upheld the Municipal Assembly's December 2024 regulation. The ruling rejected the Municipality of Pristina's claim to repeal the regulation and confirmed that Pastrimi has billing authority ([KOHA March 2026](https://www.koha.net/en/lajmet-e-mbremjes-ktv/pastrimi-kerkon-nga-komuna-tia-ktheje-parate-qe-i-mori-nga-qytetaret); [KOHA June 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/lajmet-e-mbremjes-ktv/punetoret-e-krm-pastrimi-sdalin-ne-terren-sduan-ti-ndajne-punet-me-kompani-te-tjera)). This decision ended the worker strikes and forced the Municipality to return collected funds to Pastrimi via mediation on June 25, 2025.

#### Ombudsperson Institution of Kosovo

**Hilmi Jashari**, Ombudsperson. Investigated the May 2025 waste management crisis. Convened a meeting on May 19, 2025, between Pastrimi management and the Municipality. CEO Reçica attended; Mayor Rama did not. Jashari publicly criticized the Municipality's absence as "disrespect for this institution" showing "complete irresponsibility of the representatives of the municipality in relation to the interests and concerns of the citizens of Pristina as well as of more than 800 employees" of Pastrimi ([Ombudsperson statement, May 2025](https://oik-rks.org/en/2025/05/19/the-ombudsperson-met-with-the-ceo-of-the-company-pastrimi-the-mayor-of-pristina-was-absent/)).

Jashari has a long history of environmental rights advocacy. He warned years ago that Mirash could explode like the 1993 Turkey landfill disaster ([Prishtina Insight 2017](https://prishtinainsight.com/mirash-landfill-looming-environmental-disaster-mag/)) and has led environmental marches in Pristina with civil society partners ([Prishtina Insight 2019](https://prishtinainsight.com/protesters-stand-up-for-environmental-rights/)). He has commented that "the prosecution and the courts are not giving priority to any cases that are related to the environment."

### Political parties and the underlying alignment

The Pastrimi dispute is not just an institutional fight; it tracks Kosovo's national party politics ([KOHA April 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/qytetaret-ne-prishtine-vazhdojne-te-jene-ne-dileme-se-ku-ti-paguajne-tarifat-e-mbeturinave)).

- **PDK** (Democratic Party of Kosovo). Center-right. Was in opposition nationally during much of the dispute. Pastrimi management is politically aligned with PDK. The Municipal Assembly bloc that passed the December 2024 regulation was PDK-led, with VV support.
- **LDK** (Democratic League of Kosovo). Center-right. Mayor Rama's party. Holds Pristina mayor's office but lacks Assembly majority.
- **VV / Vetëvendosje** (Self-Determination Movement). Left-nationalist. Held national government during much of the dispute under Albin Kurti. Supported PDK's December 2024 regulation in the Pristina Assembly despite being VV's national rival.
- **AAK** (Alliance for the Future of Kosovo). Smaller party, generally aligned with LDK on this issue.

The cross-cutting alignment (national VV opposing local VV's coalition behavior, etc.) is part of why the dispute has been so durable: there is no clean national-vs-local fix because the parties' interests at different levels do not align.

### Commercial generators

Restaurants, cafes, shops, construction sites. Often use residential containers in violation of regulations. Construction sites are the largest single source of illegal dumping (CDW along roads and rivers). Commercial generators in some contexts pay separate higher fees but enforcement is uneven.

The construction and demolition waste situation is particularly well-documented in academic literature. The 2023 TU Berlin / GIZ study found that construction sites use multiple disposal pathways: transportation by the construction company itself, by subcontracted private companies, via specialized contracts with Pastrimi, or via informal collection. Pastrimi has reported illegal CDW dumping practices to authorities, providing names and addresses, but no further action has been taken ([Springer 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10163-022-01577-5)).

### The informal sector

Scrap collectors as described above. A meaningful labor force, predominantly RAE community. Economically essential to the limited recycling that does happen. Politically invisible. Sometimes blamed for litter spread around bins. Detailed in the Prishtina Insight feature ([2023](https://prishtinainsight.com/waste-warriors-of-necessity-informal-waste-pickers-carving-a-livelihood-from-scraps-mag/)).

## Part 3: The legal architecture

### The Law on Waste

**Kosovo Waste Law No. 04/L-060** of 24 May 2012. This is the national-level statute governing waste management. It establishes:

- Local governments (municipalities) are responsible for waste management programs in their territory
- Waste collection, transportation, treatment, storage, disposal, import, export, environmental management plans, and the rights and obligations of licensed entities are all regulated under this law
- Municipalities are obliged to issue a register of illegal landfills in their territory within one year (this obligation has been only partially implemented)
- Licensed operators are required for waste collection and transportation as a commercial activity

The law is widely assessed as inadequate for enforcement. The European Commission (2013) cited it as an example of how Kosovo's laws lack enforcement capacity ([referenced in academic literature on EU waste enforcement](https://ebrary.net/69437/environment/illegal_waste_kosovo)). It has not been substantially updated to align with the EU Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC, amended 2018).

### The Pristina municipal regulation

The **Waste Management Regulation No. 01-030/01-227581/23** of December 1, 2023, as amended by regulation no. 01-030/01-161704/24 of December 6, 2024. The amendment is the operative legal instrument in the current dispute ([referenced in KRM Pastrimi statement, KOHA April 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/krm-pastrimi-thote-se-shkaku-i-thirrjeve-te-komunes-se-prishtines-borxhi-ka-arritur-ne-22-milione-euro)).

It specifies:
- Pastrimi has exclusive billing authority for waste services in Pristina territory
- Citizens are to pay Pastrimi directly
- The Municipality cannot collect waste fees on Pastrimi's behalf
- Private operators are to be removed from waste collection in Pristina (the regulation effectively cancels the Municipality's planned tender for five private operators)

This regulation was passed by the Municipal Assembly on December 5, 2024, by an opposition bloc (PDK with VV support) over the objections of the LDK-aligned Mayor and his administration.

**Note for research**: The regulation text in Albanian is held by the Pristina Municipal Assembly. Direct access to the December 2024 amendment text would meaningfully improve accuracy. Possible routes: the Assembly secretariat directly, civil society policy organizations (Atlas Institute, GAP Institute, FLOSSK), or BIRN's investigative desk.

### The Constitution and environmental rights

Article 52 of the Kosovo Constitution recognizes the right to a healthy environment and obligates state institutions to protect it. Kosovo's constitution incorporates the European Convention on Human Rights directly (Article 22), making ECHR jurisprudence applicable. ECHR cases including López Ostra v. Spain (1994) and Fadeyeva v. Russia (2005) establish that severe environmental pollution from waste management can violate Article 8 of the Convention (right to private and family life).

A constitutional challenge to the December 2024 regulation on environmental rights grounds has not been filed but is legally available to civil society organizations.

### Kosovo Integrated Waste Management Strategy 2021-2030

Adopted by MMPHI in May 2021. Sets ten-year targets for waste reduction, recycling, treatment, and circular economy transition ([US Trade.gov](https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/kosovo-waste-management-and-recycling)). Implementation has lagged the targets significantly. The 2010-2020 strategy that preceded it had set a 100% waste collection target by 2020; actual achievement was approximately 85% for households, 54% for businesses ([Heinrich Böll 2022](https://rs.boell.org/en/2022/08/03/kosovos-waste-mismanagement-failures-alternatives)).

### EU Waste Framework Directive

Directive 2008/98/EC, amended 2018. The relevant EU framework Kosovo waste legislation is supposed to align with. Key elements: polluter-pays principle, waste hierarchy, extended producer responsibility, pay-as-you-throw, separate collection requirements.

Kosovo's compliance with these directives is partial at best. The EU progress reports flag this annually.

## Part 4: The 2024-2025 crisis timeline

*The most comprehensive single timeline summary is the [KOHA January 2026 retrospective](https://www.koha.net/en/038-ktv/pa-buxhet-e-me-protesta-viti-2025-per-prishtinen) covering all of 2025 in Pristina including waste, transit, and budget crises.*

### Pre-crisis context (2023-2024)

In June 2023, Pastrimi reportedly completed a registration update and submitted approximately 25,000 new clients to the Municipality for inclusion in the registry. The Municipality did not act on this submission ([KOHA January 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/prishtinasit-do-te-faturohen-dyfish-per-mbeturinat)).

During 2024, Pastrimi estimated the Municipality's failure to register clients cost the company 3.5 million euros in foregone revenue.

The Municipality issued a tender for five private waste operators in 2024, intended to break Pastrimi's de facto monopoly.

### December 2024: The regulation passes

December 5, 2024. The Pristina Municipal Assembly votes to amend the waste management regulation. PDK and VV vote in favor. LDK opposes. The amendment grants Pastrimi exclusive billing authority and effectively cancels the Municipality's private operator tender.

### January 2025: Dual billing chaos

January 1, 2025. Pastrimi begins billing citizens directly under the new regulation. The Municipality continues issuing its own waste bills and refuses to recognize Pastrimi's authority ([KOHA January 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/prishtinasit-do-te-faturohen-dyfish-per-mbeturinat)).

Citizens receive duplicate bills. Confusion about where to pay is widespread.

### February 2025: Emergency declaration

February 5, 2025. The Municipality of Pristina formally announces via Facebook that citizens are not obligated to pay Pastrimi invoices issued from January 1, 2025, and directs them to pay the Municipality instead ([Telegrafi February 2025](https://telegrafi.com/en/qytetaret-e-prishtines-njoftohen-mbi-pagesat-per-grumbullimin-e-mbeturinave/); [Cna.al February 2025](https://www.cna.al/english/kosova-bota/komuna-dhe-pastrimi-perplasen-per-faturat-e-grumbullimit-te-mbeturin-i422082)).

Pastrimi calls the Municipality's announcement "illegal and arbitrary" and accuses the Municipality of trying to bankrupt Pastrimi to justify the 16-million-euro private operator tender.

Mid-February 2025. Pastrimi halts collection for several days in protest at the Municipality's failure to pay debts (the company alleged 2.7 million euros owed at this point). Trash accumulates on Pristina streets.

The Municipality declares a state of emergency in waste collection. Mayor Rama cites "significant risk of a public health crisis" to justify the emergency ([Balkan Insight 2025](https://balkaninsight.com/2025/11/28/unbearable-the-legal-and-illegal-landfills-harming-lives-and-land-in-kosovo/)).

### March-April 2025: Escalation

April 2025. By the start of April, over 21,000 citizens have made payments to the Municipality rather than Pastrimi ([KOHA March 2026](https://www.koha.net/en/lajmet-e-mbremjes-ktv/pastrimi-kerkon-nga-komuna-tia-ktheje-parate-qe-i-mori-nga-qytetaret)).

Pastrimi alleges the unpaid debt has reached 2 million euros (early April), then 2.2 million (end of April), then approximately 2.5 million (later). The KRM Pastrimi formal statement ([KOHA April 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/arberi/krm-pastrimi-thote-se-shkaku-i-thirrjeve-te-komunes-se-prishtines-borxhi-ka-arritur-ne-22-milione-euro)) details the situation.

Reçica gives the [extended Telegrafi interview](https://telegrafi.com/en/mospajtimet-se-ku-duhet-te-paguhen-faturat-e-mbeturinave-ne-prishtine-flet-kryeshefi-ndermarrjes-pastrimi/) (late April) framing the dispute as the Municipality attempting to replace Pastrimi with private operators.

### May 2025: Worker strikes and Ombudsperson intervention

May 2025. Pastrimi workers strike, protesting both the Municipality's withholding of payments and the contracting of private operators. Strike action sustained for about a month, with workers protesting in front of the Municipality of Pristina ([KOHA January 2026 retrospective](https://www.koha.net/en/038-ktv/pa-buxhet-e-me-protesta-viti-2025-per-prishtinen)).

The Municipality of Pristina hired two private companies to remove the garbage during the strike, "but they also failed to do the job properly," per the KOHA retrospective. Citizens' complaints about the stench from non-collection continued during high-temperature days.

May 19, 2025. Ombudsperson Hilmi Jashari convenes a meeting between Pastrimi (CEO Reçica) and the Municipality (Mayor Rama). Reçica attends. Rama does not. The Ombudsperson publicly criticizes the Municipality for absence and notes Pastrimi is "on the verge of bankruptcy" and that the situation endangers public health ([Ombudsperson statement May 19, 2025](https://oik-rks.org/en/2025/05/19/the-ombudsperson-met-with-the-ceo-of-the-company-pastrimi-the-mayor-of-pristina-was-absent/)).

### June 2025: Supreme Court ruling and partial resolution

Early June 2025. The Supreme Court of Kosovo rules in favor of the regulation. The ruling rejects the Municipality of Pristina's claim to repeal the December 2024 regulation. The regulation stands.

June 24, 2025. Even with the Supreme Court ruling and mediation underway, Pastrimi workers protest in front of the company building. They oppose continued operation of the private operators contracted by the Municipality during the emergency. Worker Adnan Sinani quoted to [KOHA June 2025](https://www.koha.net/en/lajmet-e-mbremjes-ktv/punetoret-e-krm-pastrimi-sdalin-ne-terren-sduan-ti-ndajne-punet-me-kompani-te-tjera).

June 25, 2025. The Municipality of Pristina, through mediation, returns the debt of collected funds to Pastrimi following the Supreme Court ruling. Worker strike ends ([KOHA January 2026 retrospective](https://www.koha.net/en/038-ktv/pa-buxhet-e-me-protesta-viti-2025-per-prishtinen)).

### Late 2025 and beyond: Unstable equilibrium

The regulation stands. Pastrimi bills directly. The Municipality has not formally accepted the new arrangement and has not canceled the private operator tender on paper. The relationship remains adversarial.

Mayor Rama wins the late-2025 runoff election but as of January 2026 has not yet presented his governing cabinet.

The KLMC debt situation continues. Pastrimi reportedly owes KLMC approximately 1.9 million euros. Mirash continues operating beyond capacity.

EU progress reports continue to flag waste management as a problem area without specific conditional pressure.

Summer 2026 (high temperatures, peak waste generation) is a plausible flashpoint for the next acute crisis.

## Part 5: Structural pathologies

*This section is analytical synthesis rather than direct citation. Patterns described are visible across the cited sources.*

### The captured monopoly

The system has the worst features of both public utility and private monopoly with neither's accountability mechanisms. Pastrimi has exclusive territorial rights without performance contracts. The "owner" (Municipality) has shareholding without operational control. The political alignment (Pastrimi management with PDK; Municipality leadership with LDK) means the shareholder relationship is adversarial rather than supervisory.

### The mutual veto

Every actor has enough power to block change and not enough to force it. The Municipality can withhold funds and make life difficult for Pastrimi but cannot dissolve the regulation. The Assembly can pass regulations but cannot enforce them when the Municipality refuses to comply. Pastrimi can go on strike or threaten bankruptcy but cannot operate without Municipality cooperation. KLMC can refuse trucks but depends on Pastrimi for revenue. Workers can strike but their union is politically aligned with the very faction protecting their employer's monopoly.

### The accountability vacuum

There is no independent regulator for waste services analogous to ARRU (water) or ERO (energy). MMPHI has licensing authority but does not use it for performance enforcement. AMMK monitors but does not enforce. The Ombudsperson can investigate but cannot sanction. Police can fine generators but not the operator. Municipal inspectors are politically constrained from fining the public enterprise their Municipality owns.

### The financing pathology

Flat-fee billing produces no volume-based incentive. Pastrimi has no margin reason to encourage waste reduction or recycling. Citizens have no reason to reduce. Commercial generators get effectively subsidized by households. The "polluter pays" principle is nominally accepted but operationally absent.

### The captured registry

The 23,000-client gap between Pastrimi and the Municipality is not random. It represents either deliberate non-registration by the Municipality (as Pastrimi alleges) or genuine administrative dysfunction (as the Municipality might frame it). Either way, it produces systematic underfunding of Pastrimi by the actor that controls registration.

### The path-dependent landfills

Mirash should have closed a decade ago. Closure plans exist. Funding for a new landfill exists. Political will to actually close Mirash and absorb the political cost of construction nearby has not existed.

### The illegal dumping equilibrium

Hundreds to over 1,500 illegal dumpsites exist nationally. Some are remediated, more are created. The construction sector generates most of the volume. Municipalities have neither resources nor political will to enforce.

### EU accession conditionality

EU progress reports identify specific waste management failures every year. Kosovo's accession progress is contingent on these reforms in theory. In practice, the EU's leverage is rarely deployed on this granular an issue.

### The information asymmetry

Citizens cannot accurately assess Pastrimi's performance because no independent measurement exists. The actors with information have political incentives to misrepresent. The actors with monitoring authority report but do not measure operationally.

### Timeline pacing

Real-world response times in the 2024-2025 dispute, useful for understanding how fast (or slow) the system moves under stress:

- Regulation passed to first billing: ~4 weeks (Dec 2024 to Jan 2025)
- First billing to first emergency declaration: ~5 weeks (Jan to mid-Feb 2025)
- Emergency declaration to court ruling: ~4 months (Feb to Jun 2025)
- Court ruling to mediated resolution: ~3 weeks (early June to June 25)
- Mediated resolution to next worker action: ~hours (workers protested same day)

The full crisis cycle ran roughly six months, from regulation passage in early December 2024 through mediated resolution in late June 2025.

## Part 8: Open questions and gaps

These are questions that the available source material does not fully answer and that may need primary research or expert outreach to resolve:

1. **The exact legal mechanism for Constitutional Court referral.** The Municipality reportedly sent the regulation to Constitutional Court but the case status is unclear in available sources.

2. **The fate of the private operator tender.** It was issued, then technically nullified by the regulation, then partially revived during the emergency. Current status unclear.

3. **Pastrimi's actual operational metrics.** Pickup frequency by neighborhood, response times, tonnage collected. None of this is publicly reported.

4. **The full client register dispute.** Why specifically the 23,000-client gap? Is it new construction? Informal settlements?

5. **The recycling sector specifics.** Izolimi Plast, Kosovo Recycling Glass, and Rec-Kos are named, but the broader landscape is poorly documented.

6. **The political alignment details.** PDK-VV cooperation in the Pristina Assembly contradicts national PDK-VV opposition. The exact horse-trading is not in available sources.

7. **The KEK relationship.** Mirash sits on KEK-owned land. The relationship between waste operations, the coal power plants, and KEK as landowner is not deeply explored.

8. **The international donor landscape.** EU funded the original landfills. JICA funds trucks. GIZ has done assessment work. Other donors (USAID, World Bank, Swiss SDC) presumably have programs.

9. **The Albanian-language regulatory text.** All references to specific regulations rely on translated summaries. Direct access to the December 2024 amendment text in Albanian would matter.

10. **The local Pastrimi culture.** Internal dynamics, depot locations, neighborhood-level stories. Would require direct outreach.

11. **The 2017 Mirash crisis vs the 2025 collection crisis.** The connection between landfill failure and collection failure is institutional but not narratively connected in available sources.

12. **The Kosovo Integrated Waste Management Strategy 2021-2030 implementation.** What targets has it hit? The strategy document itself should be retrievable from MMPHI.
